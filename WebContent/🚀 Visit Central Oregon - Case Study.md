# Find Your Center

**A regional tourism platform cannot rely on awareness alone. It has to answer intent before the visitor loses interest.**

> &nbsp;
> **Asset → Hero**
>

The right side should reveal the architecture underneath: 12 cities, 16 activity categories, and intent-driven pathways branching from searches like “hiking in Bend,” “restaurants in Sunriver,” and “camping near Sisters.” The impression should be calm, premium, and immediately legible: a beautiful region made searchable.
> &nbsp;

Visit Central Oregon was not suffering from a lack of product. The region had scenic beauty, outdoor access, food and drink, events, trails, small towns, and the kind of geographic variety destination marketers usually dream about. The issue was colder than that. People were interested, but the site was not structured around how that interest appeared.

The data made the gap visible. Survey research across 1,618 travelers in Los Angeles, Phoenix, San Francisco, and Seattle found that 40% of potential visitors were unfamiliar with Central Oregon, while 30.8% named “I don’t know enough about it” as the top barrier to visiting. At the same time, SEO research showed 716,000 monthly travel-intent searches and a 9% year-over-year increase.

Demand was there. Curiosity was there. The architecture was not catching it.

This was the central design problem: turn a regional destination website into a discovery engine. Not by adding more pages. Not by hiding the complexity behind prettier navigation. By rebuilding the information model around the exact language travelers were already using.

## The Map Was Not the Territory

**Travelers were not searching for the organization’s mental model. They were searching for what they wanted to do, and where they could do it.**

Two research streams pointed to the same structural failure. The survey data showed that the region was desirable but under-understood. Central Oregon scored high for scenic beauty at 81.8% and outdoor recreation at 76.3%, which meant the destination had strong raw appeal. The problem was not persuasion. It was access.

The keyword research explained the mechanism. Across 20,000+ keyword combinations, travelers consistently searched in a City × Activity pattern. They were not beginning with the phrase “Central Oregon.” They were beginning with intent: hiking in Bend, restaurants in Sunriver, camping near Sisters, trails near Terrebonne.

That pattern changed the shape of the entire product. If travelers think in City × Activity combinations, the platform had to do the same. Navigation could no longer be a static brochure organized around stakeholder categories. It had to become an intent resolver.

The information architecture became the strategy. Every major template, pathway, label, and content relationship needed to help a visitor move from a broad desire to a specific place, activity, business, itinerary, or event.

## Bend Was the Door

**The anchor city could either consume the region or open it. The architecture had to make it a gateway.**

Bend dominated more than 60% of the region’s non-brand search landscape. That created a real strategic tension. Bury Bend and the site would fight user behavior. Over-prioritize Bend and the other 11 cities would remain peripheral, limiting the regional economic mission the platform was built to serve.

The solution was not to balance every city equally on the surface. Equal exposure would have been politically clean but behaviorally wrong. Visitors were already using Bend as their mental entry point. The system needed to accept that reality, then redirect it into a wider regional journey.

Activity-first navigation solved the problem. A visitor searching for hiking could land in an experience that included Bend, Sisters, Terrebonne, and surrounding areas without forcing them to understand the region first. Bend remained visible because user demand required it. But it stopped acting as a ceiling.

That shift changed the political and product logic at the same time. High-traffic destinations no longer competed with smaller cities. They amplified them. The region became a connected system rather than a collection of isolated pages.

## Search Became the Structure

**The taxonomy did not describe content. It generated the product.**

Once City × Activity became the organizing primitive, the scale of the system became clear. Twelve cities multiplied across activity categories created 195 unique combinations. Those pages could not be designed as one-off editorial layouts. They needed a content architecture that could scale without becoming generic.

The system was built around six modular templates: Foundation, City, Activity, Business Listing, Itinerary, and Event. Each template had a distinct job, but all of them followed the same behavioral pattern. Meet the visitor’s intent immediately. Confirm they are in the right place. Then provide natural paths into deeper exploration.

This was where the screen became the last step. Before the interface could be refined, the content relationships had to be defined. Cities, activities, businesses, events, itineraries, categories, and subcategories all needed to interlock cleanly enough for the platform to grow without collapsing into duplicated pages or dead ends.

The taxonomy mapped 16 activity categories, 12 cities, and 717 category and subcategory combinations drawn from Travel Oregon’s established classification system. The design work was not just layout. It was the creation of a regional discovery logic that could hold hundreds of pages and still feel simple to a traveler.

## Six Templates. Seven Hundred Pages.

**Scale only works when every page knows its job.**

The legacy site contained more than 700 audited pages. That scale can quickly become a liability: duplicated content, inconsistent pathways, unclear ownership, thin destination pages, and navigation that reflects internal categories rather than visitor intent. The redesign needed to reduce cognitive load without flattening the region.

Six templates created the operating model. Foundation pages handled major orientation and brand-level storytelling. City pages gave each destination a clear identity. Activity pages organized intent. Business listings connected travelers to local economic value. Itineraries shaped decision-making into usable plans. Events added time-sensitive discovery.

The pattern across all of them was progressive disclosure. At the top of each page, the visitor needed immediate confirmation: this is the place, activity, or plan I was looking for. Below that, the system could introduce richer context, nearby options, related cities, seasonal opportunities, and conversion paths.

That structure made the platform more resilient. New content could be added without inventing a new design pattern every time. Local businesses could be surfaced in meaningful context. Regional stakeholders could see how their city fit into the whole. The system did not need to shout. It needed to hold.

## Mobile Inspired. Desktop Converted.

**The platform had to support two different travel behaviors without splitting into two different products.**

The responsive strategy followed the travel decision cycle. Mobile was trending toward inspiration-phase use, where visitors browse, compare, and explore in small bursts. Desktop still carried more booking and planning weight, where visitors review details, organize options, and make decisions with more focus.

That distinction shaped the interface. Mobile needed fast orientation, clear paths, thumb-friendly browsing, and immediate answers. Desktop needed deeper comparison, stronger content density, and conversion support. The same architecture served both, but the presentation adapted to the behavior.

Accessibility also had to live in the foundation. The custom WordPress platform was designed with ADA compliance and WCAG 2.0 AA considerations built into the structure rather than treated as a late-stage checklist. For a public destination platform, access is not a polish layer. It is part of the product promise.

In destination marketing, structure is the brand. Discoverability is the product. The interface simply makes that promise visible.

## Portfolio Card

**Visit Central Oregon: Regional Discovery Architecture**
Visit Central Oregon needed to turn travel interest into regional economic movement across a 12-city destination system. Survey research from 1,618 travelers showed that 40% of potential visitors were unfamiliar with the region, while 30.8% said they did not know enough to visit. SEO research across 20,000+ keyword combinations revealed the mechanism: travelers searched by City × Activity, not by organizational destination language. As Lead Designer at Whoa, I restructured 700+ audited pages into a scalable discovery platform built around 195 City × Activity combinations, 16 activity categories, 12 cities, and six reusable templates. The result was a custom WordPress architecture designed for search intent, regional visibility, ADA compliance, and mobile-first exploration, turning Bend from a dominant search endpoint into a gateway for the entire region.

### Headline Story

- **The problem:** Visit Central Oregon had strong travel demand, but the platform was not structured around how visitors searched or made decisions.
- **The finding:** Survey and SEO data converged on the same pattern: users wanted the region, but they lacked clear, specific access points.
- **The decision:** City × Activity became the core architectural primitive, replacing destination-first navigation with intent-first discovery.
- **The architecture:** 195 scalable City × Activity combinations were mapped across 12 cities, 16 activity categories, and 717 category and subcategory relationships.
- **The tooling:** Six modular templates governed the experience across Foundation, City, Activity, Business Listing, Itinerary, and Event pages.
- **The governance:** Progressive disclosure defined the page logic: answer intent first, then guide visitors into deeper regional exploration.
- **The adoption model:** Bend’s search dominance was reframed as a gateway strategy, allowing high-demand destinations to distribute attention across the region.
- **The outcome:** 700+ audited pages became a scalable, research-informed destination platform built for discoverability, accessibility, and regional economic growth.
