## Opening

I've spent fifteen years designing products at every stage of the lifecycle — from the first ambiguous idea to systems running at enterprise scale.

That range isn't accidental. I'm drawn to problems that are still taking shape, but I build things designed to last. The early chaos is where I'm most energized. The systems that come out of it are built to hold up long after I've moved on.

## Background

My path into product design was built through doing, not theory. A long accumulation of practical problem-solving across brands, startups, internal tools, enterprise systems, and products that didn't fit neatly into any category.

Over fifteen years I've worked with companies ranging from early-stage startups to some of the most recognized brands in the world — Walmart, Nike, Target, Honeywell, Chick-fil-A, Remington, and Bodybuilding.com among them. The work has spanned aviation avionics, sports content platforms, Web3 verification infrastructure, retail innovation, design systems, and operational tooling. The industries are different. The type of problem is consistent: complex, consequential, and rarely fully defined at the start.

I'm based in Northwest Arkansas, which keeps me grounded in the kind of real-world constraints that matter — products that have to work for field workers, businesses still running on paper, and users who won't adopt a tool that takes more than three taps. My taste and ambition, though, are not local or small.

---

## How I work

I try to find the structure underneath the visible problem.

When I look at a product, I don't only see screens. I ask what the data model is, where users get stuck, what's manual today that should be systematized, and what can be simplified without making the product weaker. Most confusing interfaces are confusing because the system underneath them was never fully resolved. Fixing the surface without fixing the system just moves the confusion somewhere else.

I'm also technically curious in a way that shapes how I design. I'll inspect the DOM, write a script, reason through an API, or map a data model when it helps me understand the problem — not to do an engineer's job, but because the design and the implementation are rarely as separate as the org chart suggests.

I've worked at every altitude: setting product direction from scratch, owning design systems at scale, embedded in engineering sprints, and leading cross-functional decisions with no clear brief. I adapt to what the problem needs, not what the process prescribes.

---

## What I'm looking for

I'm most valuable on teams where the work involves real complexity — whether that's a new product that needs its first real structure, a mature system that needs someone who can hold the whole thing in their head, or a workflow everyone knows is broken but nobody's made time to fix.

I move well between strategy and execution. I'm comfortable owning a design system and comfortable throwing one out when it's calcified into the wrong shape. I like ambiguity not because it's easy but because it's where the most important decisions get made — and where having good judgment matters most.

## A few other things

I took flying lessons to understand what it feels like to use the avionics I was redesigning under actual flight conditions. I've built operational systems for electrical contractors because the software that existed wasn't designed for people standing on job sites. I've written scripts at midnight to solve workflow problems because building the missing tool was faster than waiting for one to exist.

I care about work that has consequences. Products where clarity matters because the cost of confusion is high. Systems that help people do real things in the real world.
