## 1. Core Positioning Statement

**Caleb specializes in turning unclear, early-stage, or "bad-sounding" ideas into real, shippable product systems—without sanding off their edge.**

He works in the space between *what if* and *why not* — where a roadmap doesn't exist yet, the brief is half a sentence, and shipping is still the expectation.

---

## 2. What He Specializes In

Caleb operates at the 0→1 phase of product development. Not the polish phase. Not the iteration phase. The part where the idea is still weird enough to scare people — and someone needs to make it workable.

Specifically:
- **0→1 product clarity** — taking raw, undefined ideas and building systems that can actually ship
- **Design systems ownership** — building the foundational layer that makes everyone else's work better
- **Ambiguity navigation** — working without a full brief, making high-leverage decisions under uncertainty
- **Design ↔ development bridging** — operating comfortably between design intent and engineering constraints
- **Systems thinking over screen-level thinking** — designing the rules, not just the interface

---

## 3. How He Thinks About Challenges

Caleb doesn't start with solutions. He starts with what's actually broken — the constraint, the assumption, the structural problem nobody named yet.

He moves fast because he's not waiting for certainty. He tests because he'd rather learn from a broken prototype than a polished deck. And he keeps the weird parts — because clarity shouldn't come at the cost of character.

He's been told he finds solutions that others "never would have come up with in a million years." That's not an accident. It's what happens when you explore without being told you have to use the approved method.

**What he doesn't do:**
- Design to fill time
- Chase process for its own sake
- Wait for a perfect brief before starting
- Sand off the edge of an idea to make it feel safe

---

## 4. Working Style & Collaboration

Caleb works best when the problem isn't fully defined yet. He reads ambiguity not as a blocker, but as the job.

He's the kind of collaborator who:
- Takes an idea and runs with it — without needing to be managed
- Finds the cheaper, better solution even when nobody asked (sometimes to the billing department's frustration)
- Keeps a cool head under pressure, because the deadline is always real and the packages are always broken
- Builds things that other designers can actually use — systems, not one-offs
- Doesn't like "you must do it this way" — and doesn't inflict that on the teams he works with

He thrives in high-ambiguity environments. Platform teams, AI tools, dev-adjacent products, early-stage incubations — places where the work isn't figured out yet.

---

## 5. Core Design Beliefs

These are convictions, not values statements. They show up in every decision.

1. **Momentum beats perfection.** A shipped thing beats a perfect thing that never left Figma.
2. **The prototype is the argument.** Everything before it is just conversation.
3. **Clarity is designed, not discovered.** Someone has to make the decision. Make it.
4. **Half-baked is where the good stuff starts.** Slack was a failed game. Post-it Notes were a glue mistake. Get comfortable with "this sounds terrible."
5. **Systems over screens.** If it doesn't scale, it was just decoration.

I've spent the better part of my career doing work that didn't have a clear name yet.

Not because I couldn't find the right job title — because the problems I get pulled toward don't come with one. The brief is usually half a sentence. The roadmap is a question mark. The team is waiting for someone to just start.

That's where I do my best work.

I've redesigned avionics systems that pilots rely on at 8,000 feet. Built the design system that shipped across NerdWallet's entire product surface. Designed the platform for Major League Pickleball from the ground up — live on ESPN, updated in real time. Each of those started the same way: with a fuzzy problem, real constraints, and no clear path through.

I've been told I find solutions others "never would have thought of." I think that's because I don't wait to be told how it has to work. I explore. I prototype. I'd rather learn from a broken early version than a polished deck that never shipped.

I'm not a "design Twitter" designer. I don't chase trends or aesthetics for their own sake. I care about systems that scale, decisions that hold up under pressure, and products that actually change how teams work.

If you have an idea that sounds weird, a product that's half-built, or a team that's been stuck in the ambiguous phase longer than they should be — I'm probably the right person.
