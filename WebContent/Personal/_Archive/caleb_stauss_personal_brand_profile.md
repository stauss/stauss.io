When you look at a product, you do not only see screens. You ask:

What state changes matter?

Where does the content come from?

Where does the user get stuck?

What can be automated?

What can be simplified without making the product weaker?

That shows up in your Notion work for Argent Electric, where you were not just making a workspace look nice. It shows up in Tokenproof, where the product was not just a Web3 wallet-adjacent app. It was a trust system. Tokenproof positioned itself around proving ownership of tokens online and in real-world events without needing to expose the user’s actual wallet, which is fundamentally a UX problem around safety, verification, confidence, and access. fileciteturn0file0 You seem proud of this kind of work because it is complex, conditional, and high-trust.

Your problem-solving pattern is usually:

## Blind spots and recurring friction

### 1. You can over-expand before you converge

You generate a lot of ideas. That is a strength. But you can keep opening new branches before locking the core.

This shows up in product ideas, naming, domains, site structure, tools, and brand lines. You often have enough material to build five products before one has been fully scoped.

The useful question for you is not “what else could this become?” It is “what is the smallest version that proves this deserves to become anything?”

### 2. You sometimes mistake more options for more clarity

Asking for 10 more variations can help. But after a certain point, it can blur your taste. You often eventually say something like, “I need a break, I’ve been looking at this too long.” That is a sign that the exploration phase has done its job.

You may benefit from stricter decision rituals: pick three, rank them, state why, move on.

### 3. You can under-document your own value while over-documenting the system

You are good at explaining systems, but your portfolio also needs to clearly state your role, decisions, impact, and judgment. You can get so focused on the ERD, tokens, schema, or workflow that the human story gets buried.

A hiring manager needs to know: what did Caleb decide, change, simplify, prevent, accelerate, or make possible?

### 4. You can be skeptical of process in a way that needs translation

You are right to dislike fake process. But in larger organizations, process is often how trust gets built. You do not need to become rigid. You do need to show that your flexibility is disciplined.

Say: “I adapt process to the problem.”

Do not imply: “I do not follow process.”

### 5. You can move faster than stakeholders can understand

Your ability to see shortcuts and better solutions can threaten people who are invested in the current path. If you want senior roles, you need to show not only that you can find the better path, but that you can bring people with you.

Your next-level growth is probably not more creativity. It is influence.

### 6. You may undervalue polish because you are more excited by formation

You know visual craft matters. But your energy is strongest before the final 15%. That is fine, but for portfolio purposes, you need the final presentation to be excellent. If you want the 0-to-1 role, the case studies still need enough polish to prove judgment.

### 7. You sometimes want the brand to carry what the work still needs to prove

Your Stauss.Design tone is strong. But the portfolio cannot rely on voice alone. The case studies have to back it up with diagrams, decision points, product outcomes, before/after clarity, and evidence.

The brand can get attention. The work has to convert it.
