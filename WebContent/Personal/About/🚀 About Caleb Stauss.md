## Making sense of what is, so teams can build what’s next.

I’m Caleb Stauss, a product designer and design engineer based in Northwest Arkansas.

For more than 15 years, I’ve helped teams turn early ideas, complex systems, and messy product challenges into digital experiences people understand, use, and trust.

My work sits between vision and execution. I help teams define what they’re really trying to build, why it matters, how it should work, and what needs to be true before the screen ever makes sense.

Most design problems aren’t design problems. They’re structure problems. Logic gaps. Decisions that never got made explicit. My process starts by deconstructing those layers, finding the real shape of the problem, and building the system that gives the team a path forward.

I’ve worked across enterprise platforms, internal tools, live sports infrastructure, avionics interfaces, Web3 verification systems, healthcare, retail technology, and early-stage product bets. The industries change. The work usually comes down to the same thing: turning unclear complexity into something people can use.

A good screen solves the moment in front of the user. A good system helps the team solve the next hundred moments without starting over.

If you’re building something ambitious, tangled, or not yet fully defined, that’s where I do my best work.

## More Editorial Version

Most design problems aren’t design problems.

They’re structure problems. Logic gaps. Decisions that never got made explicit. The screen is usually where those issues show up, but it is rarely where they begin.

I’m Caleb Stauss, a product designer and design engineer based in Northwest Arkansas. For more than 15 years, I’ve worked across product strategy, systems architecture, interface design, and frontend execution, helping teams turn unclear complexity into digital products that hold together in the real world.

I don’t start with screens. I start with what has to be true for the screen to make sense: the workflow, the data, the business logic, the constraints, the edge cases, and the people who have to use the thing when it ships.

That range has taken me through enterprise platforms, internal tools, live sports infrastructure, avionics interfaces, Web3 verification systems, healthcare, retail technology, and early-stage product concepts. The variety matters because it keeps the perspective honest.

Design with altitude means knowing when to zoom out, when to come down, and when the right answer isn’t a screen at all.
