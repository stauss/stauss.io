The Setup

A Sport Moving at Speed

The interface is the last mile.

Major League Pickleball entered the national spotlight with a locked ESPN broadcast date, a fast-growing audience, and a digital foundation closer to a static brochure than a live sports platform.

In live sports, a score is not just content. It is operational infrastructure. One update on the court affects standings, brackets, team records, player profiles, event pages, and the broadcast experience simultaneously.

The league did not need better pages. It needed infrastructure the business could trust at game speed.

The Problem

The pieces existed. The system didn't.

Six manual edits per score change. Under broadcast pressure.

The platform had no relational data model. Scores lived as static text fields. Players, teams, media, and events existed as parallel collections with no awareness of each other. Supporting the first broadcast meant staying up nearly three days to manually override failures as they happened.

A single score change required six manual updates — the player record, the team record, the standings table, the schedule entry, the bracket position, and the event rankings — each edited by hand, in sequence, under broadcast pressure. Miss one, and the public-facing site contradicted itself within minutes.

The system didn't fail because the team was careless. It failed because it had been designed as a brochure, not a live data product.

The Decision

Stop adding surfaces. Fix the structure underneath.

The stack was a phased choice, not a permanent one.

After the first broadcast, the natural request was to keep building: new layouts, richer team pages, more content modules. Those needs were valid, but each new page would have created another place where live data could fall out of sync. The most important decision was to pause, reframe the work, and ask what the platform needed to understand about the sport before adding anything else.

Webflow alone couldn't carry the data model — it caps at 10,000 CMS entries, allows only two levels of nested relationships, and treats schema design as an afterthought. Pairing it with Airtable gave the league a relational engine it could operate without engineering support, with a clear sunset condition: stay until the sport outgrew it. The schema was the real product. The screens were simply its surface.

The Architecture

Update once. Resolve everywhere.

One record change updates six surfaces automatically.

Players connect to Teams; Teams connect to Scores; Scores cascade to Standings. Media connects to Players, Teams, and Events through real relationships, not static text fields. Get the relationships right, and one record change updates standings, brackets, player pages, team pages, and the live scoreboard simultaneously.

Get them wrong, and that same change becomes six manual edits done under broadcast pressure. The math doesn't sound dramatic until it's 9pm on a Sunday and the next match starts in eleven minutes.

The same logic governed content. A single photo, tagged once, resolves across player profiles, team pages, and event galleries with all metadata intact.

Operator Experience

What does a stressed, non-technical person need at 9pm during a live match?

Hide complexity. Don't remove control.

The operators running league operations during a broadcast weren't engineers. They were field staff entering scores and swapping rosters while a national audience watched. Every input had downstream consequences, so the system had to hide complexity without removing control.

The Matches table became the operational hub. Fixtures were pre-created with placeholder content, keeping the public site stable before bracket outcomes were known. Score entry came down to two fields. Everything downstream — wins, standings, bracket progression — calculated automatically.

A well-designed screen is easy to recognize. A well-designed system is what you notice when nothing breaks.

Live Broadcast

The hardest test wasn't the one we built for.

Accuracy isn't a UX nicety. It's a credibility floor.

Thousands of fans watched matches render in real time while franchise owners tracked their teams from courtside, suites, and phones. The audience included LeBron James, Tom Brady, Patrick Mahomes, Kevin Durant, Mark Cuban, Naomi Osaka, and Michael Phelps. When that's the room watching their teams render on the live site, every score entry has consequences.

The Season 2 draft tested the architecture under different pressure. A live draft is synchronous, public, and entirely irreversible. Every pick aired in real time, propagating instantly from a single operator entry across player profiles, team rosters, draft boards, and the public-facing event surface.

The system handled the full event with zero broadcast errors and zero engineering interventions — because the architecture was strictly relational, the draft required no new infrastructure.

Outcomes

Performance at every altitude.

Three seasons. Zero structural failures. Zero rebuilds.

The platform ran three full seasons on ESPN without a single structural failure or emergency rebuild. The Season 2 draft — synchronous, public, irreversible — completed with zero broadcast errors. The schema that powered the live scoreboard was later reused directly by PickleballTournaments and adjacent platforms in the category.

UI patterns designed for the operator experience were independently adopted by competing platforms across the sport.

Reflection

The screens were the last step.

Just like on the court, real mastery is mostly invisible.

The hardest problems on this project were never purely design problems. They were information architecture challenges with public-facing consequences. Recognizing that distinction — and being willing to work in that space rather than defaulting to screens — is what allowed the system to hold for three seasons, earn its own custom rebuild, and keep holding well beyond the initial build.

When another team can skip the blank-page phase because the foundation is already there, the work has done more than support a project. It has become infrastructure.
