The Estimate Was the System.

Building trust into the moving industry by making the variables behind cost visible, verifiable, and customer-owned — from inventory capture to carrier economics.
Handled · Moving Platform · Acquired by UniGroup
Scroll
95 Markets · 112 Agent Locations · 1,776 Moves Booked · UniGroup Acquisition
TL;DR

The Problem

The moving industry trained customers to distrust the estimate. Quotes were vague, fees appeared late, and the most important pricing variables stayed hidden until the customer had already committed.

The Platform

Handled became a mobile-first moving platform spanning customer inventory capture, move coordination, service support, and national agent configuration across 95 markets and 112 agent locations.

The Design Work

The critical decision was to treat the verified inventory as the core product primitive: a customer-confirmed source of truth connecting pricing, labor, scheduling, concierge support, and carrier economics.

The Outcome

The system supported 1,776 booked local moves, 1,843 completed services, and was acquired by UniGroup to support the national networks behind United Van Lines and Mayflower Transit.

The moving industry runs on a broken trust contract. A customer opens their home to an estimator, receives a number they cannot audit, and waits for the real cost to reveal itself later — sometimes after the furniture is already loaded. Handled was built against that pattern.

The Research

Trust broke before the truck arrived.

We ran ten 60-minute interviews with people who had used a moving company in the previous six months. The original concern was that customers might resist a computer-assisted inventory process inside their homes. The interviews told a sharper story.

Customers did not reject technology. They rejected processes they could not inspect. One participant said they wanted a professional opinion because they had no way to know how long a move would take. Another almost refused an estimator visit after already providing information because the process had become so frustrating. The frustration was not abstract — customers had already done work, but the company still held the authority. The job was not to make AI feel impressive. It was to make Handled feel inspectable.

The Decision

Rename the machine. Restore the agency.

The same technology can feel invasive or useful depending on the contract created by language and control. One of the first design moves was a language decision. "Computer vision" described the technology, but it carried the wrong emotional weight — it sounded like surveillance. "Automatic inventory" described the customer value. It made the system feel like help.

That naming shift mattered because the product was asking the user to point a camera around their home. The interface gave the customer control at every step: pan the room, see what was identified, confirm the items, edit what was wrong, add what was missed, mark what was fragile. Move forward only when the inventory felt accurate. The trust mechanism was not automation alone. It was automation with a human override.

A quote backed by confirmed inventory is no longer a guess. It is a structured claim.

The Primitive

The verified inventory became the primitive.

The most important design object in the system was not a screen. It was the verified inventory. Every item the customer confirmed became part of the operational truth of the move. That truth shaped the quote. The quote shaped labor. Labor shaped scheduling. Scheduling shaped concierge work.

Bad inventory creates bad pricing. Bad pricing creates margin risk. Margin risk becomes operational pressure. Operational pressure becomes a broken customer promise. Handled's design challenge was to stop that chain before it started — by turning the home into structured data the customer could see, correct, and own.

Outcomes

Acquired for the operating model.

The final validation was not that the app looked polished. It was that the system could scale into a national network. The UniGroup acquisition validated the deeper architecture: a verified inventory model, a pricing system grounded in real operational variables, and a configurable service layer that could support the national networks behind United Van Lines and Mayflower Transit.

Reflection

Handled was not a better moving app. It was a more believable moving system. The difference between a product interface and product infrastructure is that infrastructure survives contact with reality — with five user roles, 95 markets, and the full chaos of moving day.

The lesson from usability testing also stayed sharp: in this product, a critical error was not a UI mistake. It was any moment that could produce incorrect move data. When incorrect data could change the quote, the crew plan, the truck size, and the customer expectation, the definition of "correct" had to cover the whole system, not just the screen.
